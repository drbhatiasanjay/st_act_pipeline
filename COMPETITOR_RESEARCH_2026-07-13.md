### Item-by-item comparison

**1. Windowed, background-subtracted centroid refinement**
Our current `extract_peaks_from_volume()` computes each peak's centroid via
`ndimage.center_of_mass(is_peak, labeled, ...)` — a binary-mask-weighted centroid over the
*tied-equal connected component* the NMS produces. `DEFERRED_IMPROVEMENTS.md` item 0 already
proved this construction makes any intensity-weighting over that same tied region a mathematical
no-op (tied voxels are provably identical in value). The competitor technique is not "intensity-
weight the same region" — it's a **separate post-processing step over a small window (1,3,3)
voxels *around* the already-found peak**, subtracting the local 20th-percentile background first
and computing center-of-mass over the positive residual, capped at a 2.8µm max shift. This is
*exactly* the escape hatch `DEFERRED_IMPROVEMENTS.md` itself predicted would be needed ("a local
intensity-weighted refinement computed over a small window around each detected peak... as a
genuinely separate post-processing step") — now seen validated in two independent real
submissions, not merely hypothesized. One open design question neither notebook needs to deal
with the same way we do: our anisotropy is `(4.0, 1.0, 1.0)` (Z:Y:X) with real Z voxels 4x
physically larger than Y/X — a flat `(1,3,3)` window's physical footprint should be checked for
sanity against our real voxel scale before reuse, not copied verbatim.
*Gated on:* the `DetectionLoss` fix landing first — our current checkpoint predicts zero
detections, so there's nothing to refine yet. Buildable now; only measurable after that fix.
*Effort:* low. *Risk:* low (bounded shift cap).

**2. Synthetic node insertion for 1-frame gaps** and **3. Component-based short-track pruning**
(analyzed together: verified both techniques actually co-occur in *both* cited notebooks —
`yusuketogashi/lb897-baseline` (`L_min=4`) and `yaroslavkholmirzayev/v4-unet-ilp-reproduction`

Our current gap-closing (`tracker.py` L84-107) only links *existing* detected nodes across a

copy; our own track-length/density distribution likely differs from theirs.

**Important score-mechanics interaction neither notebook has to reason about for us specifically:**

*Effort:* very low (one function, dozens of lines, unit-testable with synthetic tracks right now).
*Recommend building this first, ahead of everything else on this list* — it doesn't need to wait

**5. Linear momentum (constant-velocity) extrapolation for linking**
Given the corrected framing above (we have zero motion signal today, not a failing learned one),
this is the most architecturally invasive of the five, for a reason Gemini's "add it inside the
cost calculations" framing glossed over: our tracker solves the *entire* spatio-temporal graph in
one global ILP simultaneously, so there is no "already-known t-1 assignment" available at
cost-computation time the way a sequential/greedy linker (which is what the competitor notebook
actually is here) has. A track's velocity is only knowable *after* the ILP has already solved
frame t-1→t, which is too late to feed back into that same solve's t→t+1 costs. Porting this
cleanly would need either a two-pass solve (solve once, extract implied velocities, re-solve with
velocity-augmented costs) or reformulating per-node cost to use a soft prior over incoming
candidate edges' implied velocities, rather than the precomputed hard value the competitor's
sequential linker can just look up. Worth noting: that same competitor blends velocity
extrapolation (`weight=0.50`) *with* a learned-edge bonus (`weight=0.78`), i.e. even they don't use
velocity as a replacement for a learned signal — it's a complementary geometric prior.
*Effort:* medium-high, real architectural mismatch to resolve, not a parameter add.
*Recommend sequencing this last*, after 4, 3, and 1 are in and validated.
